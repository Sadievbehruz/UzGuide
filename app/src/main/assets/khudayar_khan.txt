<b>Palace of Khudayar Khan: The Zenith of Fergana Architecture and the Last Monumental Legacy of the Kokand Khanate</b>

<b>The Palace of Khudayar Khan</b> (The Pearl of Kokand) stands prominently at the center of Kokand, one of the most ancient cultural and intellectual hubs in the Fergana Valley. It represents one of the most opulent, visually striking, and structurally complex royal compounds in the entire history of Central Asian Islamic architecture. Commissioned by the final sovereign of the Kokand Khanate, Sayyid Muhammad Khudayar Khan, this grand palace acted as the ultimate material manifestation of statehood, political authority, and administrative dominance. Characterized by its vibrantly tiled external facades, towering multi-tiered minarets, and breathtakingly gilded interior chambers, the complex serves as the modern hallmark of the Fergana architectural tradition, preserved under stringent state protection as a vital asset of global heritage tourism.

<b>The foundational history of the palace and the structural mastery of Mir Ubaydulloh</b> are deeply intertwined with the volatile socioeconomic and geopolitical transformations of the mid-19th century. Construction of the monument commenced in 1863 and reached its ultimate completion in 1871, a mere five years before the definitive collapse and annexation of the Kokand Khanate. The master architect and chief engineer supervising this monumental project was the legendary <b>Mir Ubaydulloh</b>. He strategically mobilized the elite ceramicists, plasterers, and woodcarvers from Kokand, Namangan, Margilan, and Andijan. The palace compound initially spanned an expansive area of nearly 4 hectares, erected upon a massive, artificially elevated earthen platform designed to impart a dramatic vertical scale and enhance the defensive profile of the citadel.

<b>The architectural composition, room hierarchy, and imperial spatial logistics</b> explicitly reflected the absolute sovereignty and administrative control of the ruler. According to primary structural archives, the palace originally possessed an immense spatial volume, encompassing 7 internal ceremonial courtyards and 119 distinct chambers. These rooms were mathematically segregated according to rigid state hierarchies: the central reception hall (salomxona), the supreme administrative chancellery (devonxona), petition chambers, the private imperial harem, and the heavily fortified state treasury. Today, only 2 central courtyards and 19 highly decorated chambers survive. The monumental entrance portal (pishtaq), flanked by two robust decorated minarets and wrapped in vibrant glazed tiles, constitutes the primary visual core of the modern monument.

<b>The art of glazed majolica and the distinct aesthetic of the Fergana decorative school</b> achieved their absolute creative zenith across the exterior envelope of the palace. The main facade is completely blanketed in intricate geometric patterns utilizing rich yellow, emerald green, turquoise, and deep cobalt tiles—a color palette that diverges noticeably from the sapphire-dominated architectures of Khiva and Bukhara. Elegant bands of Nastaliq calligraphy and Quranic inscriptions are seamlessly integrated across the upper brickwork, displaying aphorisms that praise the state and appeal for absolute justice. The interior ceilings present a breathtaking display of traditional Uzbek woodcarving and Kundal art (a highly specialized relief painting technique utilizing real gold leaf), retaining their intense chromatic brilliance across the centuries.

<b>The termination of the Kokand Khanate, colonial destruction, and the preservation of the citadel</b> form a deeply dramatic historical chapter within the palace walls. Following the military invasion by Russian imperial forces in 1876 and the official dissolution of the state, the royal palace was abruptly converted into a colonial military garrison and administrative office. During this period of heavy restructuring, massive sections of the original compound, including the extensive harem and several ceremonial courtyards, were completely demolished, and the invaluable state treasury was systematically plundered. Throughout the Soviet era, the building suffered diverse utilitarian re-purposing, yet its definitive main facade and primary historic halls were structurally rescued through the persistent efforts of dedicated local scholars and conservationists, transitioning into a museum by the mid-20th century.

<b>The contemporary strategic significance, curation of historical assets, and its position in New Uzbekistan</b> place this palace as the absolute primary state landmark of the Fergana region today. The complex currently houses the Kokand Museum of Local Lore, which preserves thousands of peerless historical artifacts, including authentic medieval weaponry, royal garments, rare manuscripts, and official state seals of the Khanate. The palace grounds also serve as the central global arena for international cultural summits, most notably the International Festival of Handicrafts. In conclusion, the Palace of Khudayar Khan remains an unshakeable monument to the depth of Uzbek statecraft history, an immortal summit of regional applied arts, and an eternal source of cultural wealth for future global generations.
