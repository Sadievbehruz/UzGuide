<b>Kukeldash Madrasah: The Immortal Spiritual Citadel of Tashkent and the Visual Symbol of Chorsu Architecture</b>

<b>The Kukeldash Madrasah</b> stands as one of the most culturally significant, monumental, and historically resonant educational monuments in Tashkent, situated at the dynamic core of the ancient Chorsu square. Erected during the golden age of the 16th century, this grand academic institution played a decisive role in the spiritual, scientific, and geopolitical evolution of the Movarounnahr region. Defined by its colossal entrance portal, expansive interior courtyard, and two-tiered residential vaulted chambers, the structure serves as the ultimate living testament to the distinct Tashkent architectural guild. Today, beautifully retaining its original functional mission, the complex operates simultaneously as an active Islamic seminary and a globally recognized protected asset of international heritage tourism under absolute state preservation.

<b>The influential persona of Qulbobo Kukeldash and the structural genesis of the madrasah</b> are intrinsically linked to the zenith of the Shaybanid dynasty's regional reign. Construction of this massive educational complex took place approximately between 1551 and 1575, under the direct administrative patronage of Darvesh Khan, the ruler of Tashkent, and his supreme vizier, the Khan’s foster-brother (Kukeldash) <b>Qulbobo Kukeldash</b>. Qulbobo was a phenomenal statesman, a dedicated scholar, and a generous patron of sciences and arts. He strategically financed this monumental complex to cement Tashkent as the definitive economic and intellectual crossroads of the Silk Road, structurally integrating the massive brick building into the outer city defense walls to act as a crucial elevated defensive bastion.

<b>The spatial engineering configuration, chamber hierarchy, and internal logistics</b> demonstrate the strict mathematical adherence to classical Central Asian Islamic structural laws. The madrasah exhibits a massive rectangular floor plan measuring 60 by 43 meters. The imposing main southern facade, oriented toward the historical market hub, is dominated by a majestic entrance portal (pishtaq) rising nearly 20 meters in vertical height. The central courtyard is lined with a structured two-story array of 38 residential cells (hujras), engineered specifically to house mudarrises (professors) and students. The northwestern interior sector features a spacious darskhona (grand lecture hall) and an integrated mosque, both beautifully adorned with complex, highly specialized ganch (plaster) relief carvings.

<b>The glazed ceramic decorations, majolica bands, and masterful calligraphic epigraphy</b> elevated the external aesthetic presence of the madrasah into a highly sophisticated artistic masterpiece. Although massive seismic activity and historical conflicts severely damaged the original external tile skins over the centuries, the surviving and meticulously restored fragments highlight the supreme skill of Tashkent craft guilds. The primary portal and flanking corner minarets are decorated with turquoise, deep azure, and pristine white glazed tiles, arranged into highly intricate combinations of islimi (floral) and girih (geometric) patterns. Elegant script bands in Thuluth and Kufic styles line the entrances, displaying sacred Quranic verses, prophetic traditions, and poetic chronograms detailing the foundation dates.

<b>Seismic tragedies, geopolitical instability during the khanate eras, and Soviet structural policies</b> left deep historical scars across the brick fabric of the madrasah. Destructive earthquakes in 1866 and 1886 caused absolute structural failure across the central portal, completely collapsing the massive vaults and the upper residential tiers. Throughout the Kokand Khanate period, the building was frequently re-purposed as a fortress, a caravanserai, and a public square execution site. Following the establishment of Soviet authority and the absolute prohibition of religious scholarship, the complex was stripped of its status and utilized as a commercial warehouse, a silk factory, and a temporary exhibition hall, escaping total demolition only through the fierce preservation efforts of local architects who initiated multi-stage restoration.

<b>The contemporary structural revival, active academic status, and its positioning in New Uzbekistan</b> have fully restored the pristine dignity and historical grandeur of this monument. Following the declaration of Uzbekistan's independence, the Kukeldash Madrasah was officially returned to the administration of the Muslim Board of Uzbekistan. Today, the historic halls function as a highly prestigious specialized Islamic secondary school, training the next generation of scholars in both theological and secular sciences. Coexisting seamlessly with the vibrant daily trade of the adjacent Chorsu bazaar, this azure-domed monument stands as an unshakeable manifest of the Uzbek people's eternal dedication to enlightenment, regional master craftsmanship, and the enduring identity of Tashkent.
