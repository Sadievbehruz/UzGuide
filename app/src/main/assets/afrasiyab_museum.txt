<b>Afrasiyab Museum: The Ancient Foundation of Samarkand and the Masterpiece of Sogdian Culture</b>

<b>The Afrasiyab Museum</b> is one of the most unique archaeological and historical sites not only in Uzbekistan but also in the entire Central Asian region. This museum is located on the territory of the ancient city of Samarkand, which today is known as the Afrasiyab archaeological site. The establishment of the museum was carried out in 1970 on the eve of the celebration of the 2500th anniversary of the city of Samarkand. The main task of this institution is to preserve, deeply study, and demonstrate to the general public and world tourists the unique artifacts found as a result of many years of scientific and archaeological research on the hills of Afrasiyab. The museum building was erected near the ancient city defense walls, reflecting a harmony of antiquity and modernity in its architectural structure. The museum exhibits fully demonstrate the life and cultural development of the ancient capital of Sogdiana from the VIII century BC until the beginning of the XIII century AD, right up to the Mongol invasion.

<b>When we speak</b> of Afrasiyab, a huge chain of clay hills located in the northeastern part of modern Samarkand rises before our eyes. According to the mythology of Eastern peoples, this city was named in honor of the ancient king of Turan, the famous hero Afrasiyab. However, historical and archaeological sources prove that this place was ancient Marakanda – the capital of the Sogdian state. At the beginning of the first millennium BC, life was boiling here, strong defense systems, craft quarters, and magnificent palaces were built. This ancient city survived the campaigns of Alexander the Great, the conquests of the Arab Caliphate, and the cultural rise during the Samanid era. However, in 1220, as a result of the brutal attack of Genghis Khan's troops and the complete destruction of the city's water supply system, life in Afrasiyab stopped forever. Later, the city's population moved to the central part of modern Samarkand, and the site of the old city remained mysterious hills for centuries. Since the end of the XIX century, systematic archaeological excavations began here, and millions of exhibits found as a result of these searches formed the basis of today's museum fund.

<b>The exhibition halls</b> of the Afrasiyab Museum are organized in chronological order, and each section introduces the visitor to the secrets of a certain era. A total of more than 22,000 exhibits are stored in the museum's fund, and each of them has its own unique history. Here are labor tools of ancient people, bronze and iron weapons, jewelry, coins of various periods, and most importantly, a magnificent collection of ceramic vessels. Especially, the statues made with high taste by Sogdian craftsmen and terracota images of goddesses reflect the religious beliefs and worldview of the people of the ancient era. Also, the museum has ancient ossuaries, special clay vessels in which, according to Zoroastrian traditions, the bones of the dead were kept, and the patterns and images on them testify to the high level of art of that period.

<b>The most famous</b> and valuable exhibit of the museum is undoubtedly the <b>frescoes of the "Hall of Ambassadors"</b>. This unique work of art was discovered in 1965 during excavations on the Afrasiyab hill, from the ruins of the palace of the Sogdian Ikshidi (ruler) Varkhuman, dating back to the VII century AD. The murals are the very peak of the Sogdian fine arts of that period, and very grand historical events are sealed in them. The paintings depict ambassadors from different countries bringing gifts to the ruler of Samarkand. In particular, in one of the pictures, one can see Chinese ambassadors, in another, representatives of Chach (Tashkent) and Tokharistan, as well as ambassadors distinguished by feathers in their headgear, who are assumed to be Korean. The murals depict not only diplomatic relations, but also luxurious ceremonies, caravans on elephants and horses, people crossing the river, and hunting scenes painted in very high precision and bright colors. These paintings are the most reliable visual source showing how extensive and powerful the international relations of Samarkand, which was the central point of the ancient Great Silk Road, were. Tourists and scientists visit this museum from different corners of the world just to see these pictures with their own eyes.

<b>Many items</b> in the Afrasiyab Museum show the place of Samarkand in global trade networks. Chinese porcelain displayed in the museum, gold and silver coins belonging to the Byzantine and Sasanian empires, as well as inscriptions written in various languages (Sogdian, Bactrian, Pahlavi, and Arabic) confirm that the city was a crossroads of cultures in its time. Due to the skill of the Sogdians in the field of trade, unique products flowed here from the most remote corners of the world. The collection of glassware in the museum deserves special attention – the technique of their making and transparency surprise even today's specialists. The remnants of ancient fabrics, in particular, clothes woven from silk and wool, show the development of the fashion and textile industry of that period.

<b>Today</b>, the Afrasiyab Museum is not only a place where exhibits are stored, but also a center of international scientific research. Leading archaeological institutes of countries such as France, Japan, Korea, and Russia, in cooperation with Uzbek scientists, are constantly working on studying the museum fund and restoring unique murals. Especially, with the support of the Government of Japan and UNESCO, major projects were implemented to preserve the murals and convert them into digital form. The museum has become one of the calling cards of Uzbekistan for foreign tourists. In the future, it is planned to further expand the museum's exhibition, using interactive technologies (virtual reality and holograms) to revive ancient Sogdian life. In conclusion, the Afrasiyab Museum is a living mirror of history that has been bringing the great cultural heritage of our ancestors to us across the centuries.
