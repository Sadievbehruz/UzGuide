<b>Ak Saray Palace: The Architectural Might of Amir Temur's Empire and the Gem of Shahrisabz</b>

<b>The Ak Saray Palace</b> is one of the most magnificent, grand, and world-famous architectural monuments erected by the great conqueror and statesman Amir Temur. This monumental palace complex was built in the city of Kesh (modern Shahrisabz), which was the birthplace of Amir Temur. The construction works of the palace began in 1380 AD, right after Sahibqiran's successful campaign to Khwarazm, and continued for more than a quarter of a century, until 1404. The palace served not only as the personal residence of Amir Temur but also as the most important political, diplomatic, and administrative center of the empire. Today, this monument is included in the UNESCO World Heritage List and continues to amaze tourists from all over the world as the peak of medieval Eastern architecture. The specific name of the palace, "Ak Saray" (White Palace), is not connected with the whiteness of its exterior appearance, but rather with the meanings of the word "ak" in the language of our people, which signifies noble, sacred, grand, and just.

<b>Amir Temur</b> aimed to demonstrate the boundless power, wealth, and cultural potential of his empire to the whole world by constructing this massive structure. According to historical sources, on the grand portal of the palace at that time, there was an inscription written in golden letters: "If you doubt our power, look at our buildings!" The most skillful architects, ganch carvers, painters, and mosaic masters from various corners of the empire, particularly from Khwarazm, Iran, Azerbaijan, Iraq, and India, were involved in the construction. Especially, the masters brought from Khwarazm applied completely new and unique methods in the tile-coating system of the building. The construction of the palace was under the personal supervision of Sahibqiran, and he paid special attention to the perfect outcome of each section.

<b>The internal and external architecture</b> of the palace was not an exaggeration to say a engineering miracle of its time. Ak Saray consisted of a huge courtyard and hundreds of rooms located around it, luxurious reception halls, state council halls (devonxona), and the harem section. In the middle of the palace courtyard, a huge pool was built, and water flowed into it from the nearby mountains, more precisely from the Taktagoracha pass, through special clay pipes. The water was ensured to fall into the pool forming a waterfall from the high roofs of the palace, which created a cool and pleasant climate in the palace territory during the hot days of summer. The foundation of the building was made very deep and strong, protected by special layers to withstand groundwater and earthquakes.

<b>The most astonishing part</b> was the massive entrance portal of the palace. The height of this portal was originally over 70 meters, and the width of its arch was more than 22 meters. Today, only the huge tower-like foundations (pillars) on both sides of this grand portal have survived, and their current height is still about 38 meters. The surface of the portal was completely covered with turquoise, blue, white, and golden tiles, reflecting complex geometric patterns, plant ornaments, and holy Quran verses, philosophical wisdoms written in Suls and Kufic scripts. The harmony of colors among the tiles radiated extremely beautiful glimmers in the sunlight and was visible even from a long distance.

<b>The Spanish ambassador</b> Ruy Gonzalez de Clavijo saw the Ak Saray complex with his own eyes in 1404 on his way to the capital of Amir Temur and left a detailed description of this miracle in his diaries. Clavijo writes that the interior decorations of the palace rooms were filled with gilded patterns, and very exquisite pictures were painted on the walls. The ambassador was particularly amazed by the luxury of the reception halls, the marvelous engineering solution of the ceilings, and the doors made of rare wood types and decorated with silver and ivory. According to his writings, Ak Saray was much more grand and beautiful than the palaces of all the European kings he had seen.

<b>As a result of the cruel tests</b> of history, this grand palace complex suffered serious damage at the end of the XVI century. The ruler of the Bukhara Khanate, Abdullah Khan II, during the process of subjugating Shahrisabz, ordered the destruction of this magnificent building reflecting the greatness of Amir Temur. Abdullah Khan wanted to erase the power of the Timurids from the people's memory by destroying Ak Saray, but the foundations and the portal of the building were so robust that it was impossible to eliminate it completely. Strong earthquakes that occurred over the centuries also affected the remains of the palace, but the huge portal pillars standing tall remained a symbol of courage and resilience.

<b>During the years of independence</b>, very extensive landscaping, archaeological study, and conservation works were carried out on the territory of Ak Saray. Old irregular buildings around the palace were removed, and a huge and beautiful national park was established. Today, a magnificent monument of our great ancestor Amir Temur stands proudly at the foot of the Ak Saray portal. Continuous work is being carried out in cooperation with international restorers and Uzbek scientists to preserve the remaining tiles and deliver them safely to future generations. The Ak Saray Palace is not just a monument of the past, but a great proof of the high statehood history, architectural genius, and cultural intellect of the Uzbek people.
