<b>Sitorai Mohi-Khosa Palace: The Ultimate Monumental Residence of Bukhara Civilization, Architectural Eclecticism, and the Chronicle of Royal Heritage</b>

<b>The Sitorai Mohi-Khosa Palace</b>, localized within the northern perimeter of the ancient oasis city of Bukhara, stands as the final, absolute, and highly sophisticated summer palace complex constructed by the Manghit dynasty, the last ruling house of the Emirate of Bukhara. Translating directly from Persian as "The Palace of Star-like and Moon-like Beauty," this monumental estate was engineered during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, executing a radical and seamless synthesis between traditional Islamic architecture and European Neo-Classicism. The structural evolution of the site represents a profound conceptual shift in the regional urban planning matrix, serving as an architectural record of intense socio-political transformations. Today, preserved under rigorous institutional directives within the Bukhara State Museum-Reserve, the palace functions as a paramount international asset for global historical tourism and elite cultural exploration.

<b>The structural syntax of eclecticism, the integration of Bukhara and St. Petersburg construction guilds, and advanced engineering physics</b> compose the baseline infrastructural layer of the monument. The royal compound is divided into two distinct historical strata: the older sector commissioned under Emir Abdulahad Khan in classical Eastern layouts, and the grand new palace engineered for the final sovereign, **Emir Alim Khan**, via a highly calculated collaboration between Russian military architects Margulis and Sakhovich and the elite master craftsmen of Bukhara. The structural shell features Western palace configurations, double-glazed window systems, and Dutch heating networks, while the interior spaces remain bound to the strict geometric laws of local ornamental carving, generating a unique material dialogue between East and West.

<b>The spatial opulence of the White Hall, Usto Shirin Muradov’s ornamental ganych genius, and the physics of mirror-reflective symmetry</b> represent the absolute zenith of the palace’s artistic interior. The most structurally complex and visually breathtaking zone within the new palace is the legendary **White Hall** (Beliy Zal), executed under the direct artistic supervision of the master craftsman and Academician **Usto Shirin Muradov**. The structural walls are lined with high-grade vertical mirror plates, overlaid with incredibly intricate, hand-carved openwork plaster lace (ganych). This specific technique prevents shadows from forming against the wall surfaces, producing a transcendent optical illusion of weightless, floating structural patterns that expand infinitely across the mirror spectrum, a feat celebrated worldwide in structural interior theory.

<b>The thermodynamics of fired brick masonry, landscape microclimate engineering, and the imperial diplomatic archive</b> anchor the deep material and historic value of the compound. The palace is enveloped by an expansive park system that integrates deep water reservoirs (hauz) and free-roaming peacocks, engineered to drastically lower ambient summer temperatures via classic thermodynamic principles. The interior exhibitions preserve a massive collection of diplomatic artifacts, including monumental Russian crystal chandeliers, massive cloisonné vases, imperial Romanov furnishings, and elite Chinese Qing dynasty porcelain. This massive archive of luxury items acts as a permanent material ledger of the Emirate's global economic networks, high-level foreign policy, and elite consumer aesthetics.

<b>The contemporary paradigm of museumification, the renaissance of traditional applied arts, and global soft-power branding in New Uzbekistan</b> has transitioned the palace into an era of deep institutional validation. Under explicit state execution for the absolute preservation of national identity, Sitorai Mohi-Khosa has undergone non-invasive structural conservation and highly advanced curation re-engineering. The specialized museums housed within the wings, focusing on Bukharan costume history and applied arts, have been updated to meet international standards of museology. Within New Uzbekistan's modern grand strategy of cultural diplomacy, this architectural masterpiece operates as a crucial international hub, demonstrating the elite intellectual wealth, artistic resistance, and historical complexity of Central Asian civilization to millions of global travelers.
