<b>Independence Square: The Chief Monumental Symbol of Uzbek Statehood, Liberty, and National Revival</b>

<b>Independence Square</b> (Mustaqillik Maydoni) stands as the most magnificent, expansive, and central public plaza in Tashkent, the capital of the Republic of Uzbekistan, serving as a living emblem of the country’s sovereignty, freedom, and bright future. This immense square functions not only as the primary political stage where high-level state ceremonies, official military parades, and major national celebrations are conducted, but also as a beloved cultural and recreational hub for capital residents and international guests alike. Covering a total geographic area of nearly 12 hectares, the territory features a peerless synthesis of majestic fountains, lush ornamental walkways, imposing administrative structures, and deeply philosophical sculptural monuments, making it a supreme manifestation of contemporary Uzbek urban design.

<b>The history of development and ideological transformation of the square</b> comprehensively reflects the sweeping political and structural changes that occurred within the country over the last two centuries. In the late 19th century, during the Tsarist Russian imperial administration, the palace of the Governor-General of Turkestan and an adjacent military parade ground occupied this location. Throughout the Soviet era, the site underwent an absolute ideological reshaping, officially named first as "Red Square" and subsequently "Lenin Square," featuring a colossal bronze statue of the Soviet leader at its geometric center. Following Uzbekistan's breakthrough realization of state sovereignty in 1991, this central territory was completely reimagined based on national values and historical memory. Imperialist and totalitarian symbols were systematically dismantled, allowing the square to acquire its contemporary free and awe-inspiring presence.

<b>The Arch of Goodness and the visual entrance of the plaza</b> welcome arriving visitors with fine architectural balance. Erected prominently at the primary approach, the pure white marble <b>Arch of Goodness</b> (Ezgulik Arkasi) is crowned by elegant sculptures of storks taking flight. These storks structurally symbolize peace, human kindness, traditional Eastern hospitality, and the nation’s dynamic ascent into the global diplomatic arena. Walking through this grand arch opens up an expansive, manicured promenade. The massive fountain arrays spanning the open spaces, originally engineered in the 1960s and 1970s and subsequently modernized with contemporary hydraulic tech, provide cooling relief during intense summer months while symbolically illustrating the eternal flow of human life.

<b>The Monument of Independence and Humanism and the Happy Mother</b> form the conceptual and philosophical core of the entire public square. Positioned upon a high granite pedestal in the central sector rises the <b>Independence Monument</b>—a massive bronze globe displaying an intentionally magnified, detailed cartographic contour of the Republic of Uzbekistan. This creative design underscores the historical reality that the resilient Uzbek people have permanently secured their sovereign, respected position on the political map of the world. At the base of this global sphere sits the moving statue of the <b>Happy Mother</b>, tenderly cradling her newborn infant with a gaze filled with love and quiet optimism. This powerful monument represents the Motherland, the state's deep commitment to the welfare of future generations, and the serene peace enjoyed by families in a stable country.

<b>The Memory Square and the Mourning Mother statue</b> evoke profound structural reverence and deep historical reflection within the human heart. Situated in the northern tier of the complex is a quiet memorial zone dedicated to the sacrifice of over half a million soldiers from Uzbekistan who perished during World War II. Long, open-air wooden galleries built in traditional regional styles feature columns intricately carved by master craftsmen; fixed along their structural walls are metal tablets listing the names of fallen soldiers in gilded lettering. At the focal point of these galleries, adjacent to an eternal flame, sits the moving monument of the <b>Mourning Mother</b>. This deeply evocative sculpture serves as an enduring tribute to the immense psychological grief, sacrifice, and unyielding fortitude of all mothers who endured the horrific consequences of war.

<b>In terms of administrative infrastructure, state authority, and modern civic life</b>, Independence Square serves as the executive heart of the nation. The plaza is flanked by the majestic structural facades of the Senate of the Oliy Majlis, the Cabinet of Ministers, and primary state ministries. Since independence, extensive botanical landscape engineering has introduced rare ornamental trees and vivid floral displays gathered from across the globe. Every year on September 1st—Independence Day—and during other prominent public holidays, the entire complex is adorned in festive lights, where state leadership and international diplomatic delegations place wreaths at the foot of the monument. In conclusion, Independence Square remains the supreme physical manifest of national dignity, an inexhaustible fountainhead of liberty, and the confident gaze of the Uzbek people toward their timeless future.
