<b>Magoki Attori Mosque: The Multi-Layered Archaeological Miracle of Bukhara and the Crossroads of Ancient Civilizations</b>

<b>The Magoki Attori Mosque</b> stands as one of the most culturally unique, historically enigmatic, and archaeologically significant monuments in the ancient oasis city of Bukhara, located immediately west of the celebrated Lyabi-Khauz square. The contemporary historical name translates to "The Mosque in the Pit of the Perfume Sellers" or simply the "Subterranean Mosque." This architectural complex departs fundamentally from other traditional regional structures due to its multi-layered, vertical stratigraphic evolution. Over the centuries, due to the continuous accumulation of urban historical debris and cultural layers, the original medieval structure was entirely swallowed by the earth. Today, preserved as a crown jewel within the UNESCO World Heritage status of Bukhara, it acts as a living document of pre-Islamic cults and early Islamic architecture.

<b>The pre-Islamic foundations and the sacred history of the sanctuary</b> reside deep within the primary geological strata of the structure. Systematic archaeological test pits have conclusively proven that long before the introduction of Islamic theology, this exact geographical coordinates hosted a highly prominent pagan temple—specifically a Zoroastrian fire-altar structure. This site was intimately connected to the historic "Mokh Bazaar" (The Moon Market), which specialized in the trade of medicinal herbs, spices, and idols. Following the early Arab conquest of Movarounnahr, the pagan shrine was systematically replaced in the 9th century by a primary Islamic mosque. According to Abu Bakr Narshakhi’s famous 10th-century text "The History of Bukhara," this shrine retained deep spiritual sanctity for both Muslims and remaining non-Islamic local factions for generations.

<b>The 12th-century southern portal and the architectural zenith of the Karakhanid Dynasty</b> constitute the supreme fine-art asset of the entire building. The original early medieval mosque structure was totally obliterated in the catastrophic city-wide fire of 937. It was subsequently engineered anew in the 12th century under the imperial patronage of the Karakhanid rulers. During this phenomenal construction campaign, the globally celebrated <b>southern portal</b> was added to the facade. This portal represents the absolute historical summit of structural brickwork design, utilizing deeply carved unglazed terracotta blocks and complex ganch (alabaster) reliefs. The interlocking geometric girih grids, fluid vegetal islimi patterns, and monumental Kufic calligraphic bands exhibit a mathematical precision that continuously captivates global historians.

<b>The spatial composition and transcendent internal atmosphere</b> are dramatically enhanced by the physical depth of the structure. During the 16th-century Bukhara Khanate era, under the sovereign direction of Abdulaziz Khan, the upper horizontal ceiling and the primary brick domes were completely overhauled, yet the exquisite 12th-century southern facade was left buried beneath the urban street level. The interior floor plan presents a rectangular layout sustained by six massive brick columns which elevate a complex vaulted dome matrix. The absolute austerity of the internal brickwork, the total lack of colorful glazed tiles, and the placement of the floor nearly 4.5 meters beneath the contemporary street level create an atmosphere of profound silence, historical weight, and deep spiritual introspection.

<b>The monumental discovery by Academician Shishkin and the structural liberation of the monument</b> rank among the most crucial archaeological breakthroughs of the 20th century. For generations, the continuous commercial growth of the surrounding bazaars had completely masked the lower half of the temple. In 1934-1935, a specialized scientific expedition led by the legendary archaeologist and architectural scholar <b>Vasily Shishkin</b> initiated deep exploratory excavations around the compound. The project successfully uncovered the buried Karakhanid masterpiece in flawless structural condition. To permanently isolate the fragile brick foundations from groundwater saturation and preserve the asset, a massive open perimeter trench was engineered, providing the mosque with its iconic modern dramatic sunken profile.

<b>The contemporary museum status, the fine curation of carpet weaving arts, and its strategic role in New Uzbekistan</b> ensure the ongoing academic vitality of the site. Today, the Magoki Attori Mosque no longer functions as a religious sanctuary; instead, it operates as the official Museum of the History of Carpet Weaving under the state curation of the Bukhara State Museum-Reserve. The specialized exhibition features rare, hand-knotted antique carpets, historical felts, and complex flat-weaves executed by Uzbek, Turkmen, Persian, and Azerbaijani master craftsmen over the centuries. The historic juxtaposition of commercial textile heritage within a sacred subterranean structure creates a powerful cultural symbiosis, standing proud in New Uzbekistan as an unshakeable monument to our civilizational roots.
