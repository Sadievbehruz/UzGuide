<b>Museum of Applied Arts of Uzbekistan: The History and Charm of National Folk Crafts</b>

<b>The Museum of Applied Arts of Uzbekistan</b> is one of the most unique spiritual centers embodying the centuries-old cultural heritage, exquisite taste, and extremely rich handicraft traditions of the Uzbek people. Located in the central part of Tashkent, this museum possesses immense architectural and historical value not only for the exhibits inside but also for the building itself. The museum building is an Oriental palace complex specially built at the end of the 19th century, more precisely between 1896 and 1904, for the Russian diplomat and statesman Alexander Polovtsev. The most skillful Uzbek folk masters, ganch carvers, and painters from Tashkent, Fergana, Samarkand, and Bukhara were involved in decorating the building during that period. For this very reason, the ganch carving and wood patterns on the walls and ceilings of the palace are considered the highest peak of national art and amaze every viewer from the first seconds.

<b>The establishment of the museum</b> officially dates back to 1927, when the first exhibition featuring the works of Uzbek folk craftsmen was organized here. Later, in 1937, this place was transformed into a permanent Museum of Applied Arts. Today, more than 7,000 highly rare and valuable exhibits are preserved in the museum fund. This treasure completely covers more than twenty directions of Uzbek folk applied art, including ancient crafts such as ganch and wood carving, ceramics, jewelry, artistic embroidery, carpet weaving, national costumes, knife-making, and metalworking. Each exhibit reflects the socio-cultural environment of its time and the high skill of the master.

<b>The exposition of exhibits</b> is perfectly arranged throughout the museum halls based on regional and stylistic principles. A person visiting the museum can clearly see the differences and unique characteristics of handicraft schools typical of various regions of Uzbekistan. For example, while the ceramics department features the attractive blue and turquoise pottery of the Rishtan and Gijduvan schools, the Khwarazm and Tashkent ceramic samples stand out for their heavy balance and unique geometric patterns. These ceramic products have preserved ancient secrets within themselves across centuries without losing their color and radiance.

<b>The artistic embroidery and national costumes</b> hall is one of the most attractive and colorful sections of the museum. Famous Suzanis of Nurata, Bukhara, Shahrisabz, Samarkand, and Tashkent dating back to the 19th and 20th centuries are displayed here. The flowers and patterns used in the Suzani of each region possess a specific symbolic meaning, expressing noble intentions such as happiness for the family, abundance, and protection from evil eyes. Also, the gold-embroidered robes of Bukhara, women's dresses made of silk and atlas, and various types of Uzbek national skullcaps (doppa) testify to how high the clothing culture of our people was.

<b>The jewelry and metal carving</b> section also stands out for its exquisiteness. Since ancient times, Uzbek jewelers used gold, silver, and various precious stones (turquoise, ruby, coral) to make extremely elegant women's ornaments. Tillaqosh (head ornaments), necklaces, bracelets, and earrings displayed in the museum surprise people with their complex execution techniques. Coppersmithing and chasing products – copper water vessels, platters, and jugs revive the luxury and cultural rise of medieval Eastern markets before our eyes. The national knives created by the masters of Chust and Shahrisabz have risen to the level of works of art due to their sharpness and the exquisite decorations on their handles.

<b>The Central Hall of the museum building</b> is the heart of the entire complex due to its architectural structure. The ceiling part and pillars of this hall were decorated by great representatives such as master Toshpolat Arslonqulov and master Shirin Murodov, and the mutual harmony of colored paints in the "vassa" style and ganch mixture gives the building a special palace atmosphere. Inscriptions in Arabic script sealed on the walls and complex ganch carving elements are important sources for studying the architectural aesthetics of that period. International experts have recognized this hall as the best-preserved example of an Oriental interior from the end of the 19th century in Central Asia.

<b>Today, the Museum of Applied Arts</b> has become not only a storehouse of historical exhibits but also a center for lively cultural events, master classes, and international exhibitions. Exhibition-sales of modern folk masters and craftsmen are frequently organized on the territory of the museum, where foreign tourists can directly see the process of making Uzbek national items with their own eyes and take lessons from master craftsmen. The museum plays an extremely important role in introducing the rich cultural tourism potential of Uzbekistan to the world and educating the younger generation in the spirit of national values. In conclusion, the Museum of Applied Arts of Uzbekistan is a symbol of the genius of our people, their boundless love for beauty, and an eternal cultural treasure.
